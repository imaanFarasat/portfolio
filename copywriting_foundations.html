Story: The Four Riders (Short Fiction)

Originally written in Persian

Genre: Psychological Fiction / Literary Fiction

Author: Iman Farasat

Overview

The Four Riders is a deeply emotional and psychologically intense short story about a young man navigating the terrifying decline of his brother's mental health. Written in Persian, the narrative explores fear, family responsibility, hallucination, and the fragile boundary between reality and imagination. Through vivid descriptions and raw honesty, the story captures a brother's struggle to protect someone he loves while losing him in a way he can neither stop nor fully understand.

Short Summary (English)

The story follows the narrator and his 28-year-old brother, Farhad, who suffers from severe anxiety, paranoia, and agoraphobia. As Farhad's mental state worsens, he becomes convinced that four men on motorcycles are stalking him, stealing his hat, and hiding inside the house. One afternoon, during a frightening hallucination, he believes he is fighting these attackers. The narrator desperately tries to break into the room to save him‚Äîbut Farhad, fully lost in his delusion, falls from the window. The authorities call it suicide. The narrator is left alone in the silence of their apartment, wearing Farhad's blood-stained hat and unable to decide whether his brother jumped, was chased by invisible enemies, or was simply defeated by his own mind.

Themes

  • Mental health & stigma
  • Family loyalty
  • Fear, paranoia & hallucination
  • Emotional weight of caregiving
  • The thin line between reality and perception

Why This Story Matters

This story demonstrates my ability to craft:

  • Emotionally powerful narratives
  • Complex character psychology
  • Scene-based tension
  • Long-form storytelling
  • Descriptive, cinematic writing

It reflects my strength as a writer who can create atmosphere, build narrative arcs, and explore human depth with honesty and precision.

Excerpt (Translated to English)

"He sat cross-legged on the pillow, his fingers hanging from his knees, eyes gently shut like a monk preparing for something sacred. When I whispered his name, he lifted a finger to his lips, asking for silence.

Moments later he leaned close to my ear and said, 'They came. Two motorcycles‚Ä¶ four men.'

I held his hand to calm him, but he pulled away. 'They took my hat,' he insisted, though the hat was still on our table.
